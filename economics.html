Economics

What our money is doing

As mentioned, this venture was attempted once before in Vancouver by PintPost; but due to various issues, they have since closed down. This company had set their price point at $40 which we at World of BeerCraft believe to be quite steep for the service being provided. After some discussions amongst the founders, we believe that a fair price for this service is $33 dollars a month. Please note that members of our recycling subscription program (refer to Sustainability) only need to pay $31 a month. As mentioned in our Market tab, we foresee through vigorous advertising, we can acquire 4227 subscribers in our first year of operation. This basis of people is 2% of our SAM and is more than serviceable by our competent delivery staff.


To assess the viability of this idea, we perform a sensitivity analysis, varying our serviceable obtainable market and price. Using a pessimistic, likely, and optimistic scenario, we investigate the expected value and risk based on the rate of return in each scenario. To achieve our pessimistic estimates, we use our most likely values ($33 subscription fee with 4227 subscribers in year 1) and while holding one value constant, see what value the other needs to be in order to break even with $0 retained earnings in our first year of operation.


Holding the price constant at $31, we found that to break even in our first year of operation is when our starting subscription base is 2863 subscribers. This value is extremely close to 1% of our SAM, 2817 subscribers so this number is used as a pessimistic estimate of subscribers.


Using 4227 subscribers in year 1, a break even in retained earnings in our first year is achieved with a subscription price of $29.67 a month. This can be thought of as an average price as some people may not be part of our recycling program. From a pessimistic viewpoint, the subscription price is when everyone is part of the recycling program which would be a price of $29.

Looking at the expected value, we get 62.94%. Calculating the risk or standard deviation of the expected value, we get a value of 12.39% which is much smaller than our expected value, indicating that this is a good venture. Though it passes the rule of thumb with the expected value being at least twice as great as risk, we should still be aware there is still a decent amount of risk associated with this project stemming from the risk subscriber base.


As seen, in our most likely outlook looking at 3% of the total craft beer drinkers who purchase their craft beer privately and enjoy drinking at home at a price of $33 a month. Aside from our most pessimistic scenario, there is no issues with meeting our MARR. We will assume the most probable scenario will occur, 3% SAM at a monthly rate of $33 when performing calculations.


This model is sensitive as it relies heavily on the amount of subscriber base that is obtained. A shift in either more or less subscribers will require an economic reevaluation causing an increase or decrease of subscription price.


Our after-tax MARR is developed by looking at the TSX over the last ten years. Looking at the growth trend in the TSX, we found there was an increase in prices by 10%, giving us a market interest rate of 10%. We set this as our after-tax market interest rate. Since our cash flow is based on real numbers (inflation is assumed to increase at the same rate as costs and benefits), we deflate the market interest to obtain a real interest rate. Please refer to the appendix for this calculation. With an average inflation rate of 1.5% over the last 10 years, we obtained an average real interest rate of 8.37% if one were to invest their money into the market instead of into our venture. Based on this real interest rate, we set our after-tax MARR at 9%, slightly above market rate to ensure we still turn a small profit even if our rate of return is equal to our MARR.
