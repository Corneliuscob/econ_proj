Costs and Before Tax Cash Flow

Sunk Costs / Monthly Expenses / Annual Expenses

$$ Sunk Cost = Q * P $$ Where \(Q \) is the Quantity of the product and \(P\) is the price of the product Sample Calculation Vehicles to drive: $$ 5 * -$30,000 = -$150,000 $$


Operating Costs

Operating costs are the sum of all the monthtly expenses and yearly costs added together. Note that all monthly costs are multiplied by 12 to represent an annual cost. $$ OC = Be + B + Ad + GI + LR + W $$ Where:

\(Be\) is the cost of beer to purchase
\(B\) is the cost of packaging each set of beers
\(Ad\) is the cost of advertising
\(GI\) is the cost of gas and insurance
\(LR\) is the cost to renew liquor licenses
\(W\) is the cost of wages to pay


Operating Revenue

The operating revenue ( OR) is the monthly cost of delivering beer multiplied by 12 $$ OR = Q * P *12 $$ Where:

\(Q\) is the number of beer packages to sell
\(P\) is the price at which beer is sold
Sample Calculation Operating Revenue year 1: $$ OR = $31*4,227*12 = $1,572,444 $$


Before Tax Cash Flow

The before tax cash flow is the sum of the operating revenue less the operating costs $$ BTCF = OR - OC $$ Where:

\(BTCF\) is the before tax cash flow
Sample Calculation BTCF year 1 : $$ BTCF = 1,572,444-1,280,606 = $291,838 $$

Sizing

Fridge Sizing

Our fridge is 6'x6'x7.5'

A typical can of beer has the dimensions of 0.21625' x 0.6170833'

A shelving unit can be placed inside the fridge every 1.5' in height.
A 2' walkway down the fridge is kept.
The number of cans placed along the length is the length of the fridge, divided by the diameter of the can (27 cans).
The number of cans placed along the width is the width of the fridge, divided by the diameter of the can (18 cans).
The number of cans per shelving level is \(18*27 = 972 \) cans.
The total number of cans that can be stored in a fridge is the cans per level times the nubmer of shelve levels: \(972*5 = 4,860\)
the number of 6-packs is the total number of cans divided by 6: \(4,860 \div 6 = 80\)

Rates

Real Rate

The real interest rate is the interest rate that does not account for inflation $$ i' = {i-f \over 1+f} $$ where (\i'\) is the real interest rate
where (\i\) is the market interest rate
where (\f\) is the inflation
Sample calculation: \(i = 0.12 ; f = 0.015 \) $$ i' = {0.12-0.015 \over 1+0.015} = 0.01034 $$

Equivalent Interest Rate

The equivalent interest rate is the inerest rate a a time period equalized from the annual interest rate $$ i_{eq} = (1+i_a)^{{c \over p }} -1 $$ where \(i_{eq}\) is the equivalent interest rate
where (\i_a\) is annual interest rate
where (\c\) is the number of compounding periods per year
where (\p\) is the number of payment periods per year
Sample calculation: \(i_a = 0.1034 ; c = 1 ; p = 12 \) $$ i_{eq} = (1+0.1034)^{{1 \over 12 }} -1 = 0.008237 $$

Loans

Loan Repayment

The loan repayment is an ordinary annualized payments. The loan term is given in payback periods of months. $$ A = P\bigg{[} {i(i+1)^n \over {(1+i)^{n-1}}} \bigg{]} $$ Where:

\(A\) is the annualized payment to pay each year
\(P\) is the amount loaned
\(i\) is the loan interest rate
\(n\) is the number of time periods correlary to the interest rate
Sample Calculation: monthly loan repayment over 60 months at monthly interest rate of 0.8%: $$ A = 200,000 \bigg{[} {0.008(1.008)^{60} \over {(1.008)^{59}}} \bigg{]} = $4,238 $$ To determine the annual loan repayment, the annualiazed payment is multiplied by 12 ($50,856).


Loan Interest

Loan interest to pay per month is the remaining amount of the loan to pay back multiplied by the loan interest rate. $$ LI = L * i $$ Where:

\(LI\) is the amount of the loan repayment towards interest \(i\) is the loan interest rate
\(L\) is the remaining amount to pay off the loan
Sample Calculation: Year 1 loan interest paid: $$ LI = 200,000*0.008 = $1,647.42 $$ The annual interest paid off is the sum of every 12 months of loan interest. E.g. Year 1 \(LI = $18,321.2\)


Loan Principle

Loan principle is the amount put towards the principle loan repayment $$ LP = A - LI $$ Where:

\(LP\) is the amount of the loan repayment towards principle \(i\) is the loan interest rate
\(L\) is the remaining amount to pay off the loan
Sample Calculation: Year 1 loan interest paid: $$ LI = 4,238 -1,647 = $2,591 $$ The annual principle paid off is the sum of every 12 months of principle repayment. E.g. Year 1 \(LI = $32,535\)

Taxes

Depreciation by CCA (Open Book)

Following Canada's tax depreciation schedule, the initial purchases of capital's depreciation is multiplied by half the CCA rate for the class $$ D_p = {CCA \over{2}} * BV $$ Where:

\(D_p\) is the depreciation in the year of the purchase of capital
\(CCA\) is the depreciation rate specified by the Canadian government
\(BV\) is the book value of capital investment In subsequent years, the Depreciation schedule follows the full CCA rate $$ D_n = CCA * BV_{n-1} $$ \(D_n\) is the depreciation in the \(n^{th} \) year
\(CCA\) is the depreciation rate specified by the Canadian government
\(BV_{n-1}\) is the book value of capital investment in the year prior to the \(n^{th} \) year Sample calculation: Year 1 and 2 Depreciation in CCA class 10 where capital is bought in year 1: $$ D_1 = 15,300*0.30/2 = $22,950 $$ $$ D_2 = 130,050*0.30 = $39,015 $$


Book Value (Open Book)

The book value in the \(n^{th} \) year \(BV_n\) is calculated by the book value of the previous year minus the depreciation $$ BV_n = BV_{n-1} - D_n $$ Sample calculation: Year 2 book value: $$ BV_2 = 150,000-22,950 = $13,0050 $$


Taxable Income

Taxable income in the \(n^{th} \) year is the income which can be taxed by the government. Drat $$ TI_n = IC_n - D_n - LI$$ Where \(IC_n\) is the income in the \(n^{th} \) year Sample Calculation: Year 1 taxable income with a class 10 and class 8 group $$ TI = 291,838 - 22,950 - 1,851 = $248,716 $$


Tax Due

Tax due is the amount of tax owed to the government in the \(n^{th} \) year $$ T_n = TI_n * Tr $$ Where Tr is the compounded provincial and government tax rate Sample Calculation: Year 1 tax due $$ T_1 = 248,716*0.12 = 29,846 $$ Note: No marginal tax rates apply for these calculations.


Tax Due on Salvage Item

Tax due on salvage is the tax due after salvaging an item. $$ TS = \sum^N_{k=1} [(SV_k - BV_{nk})*CCA_k] $$ if \(SV > SV_1\) then capital gains are taxed: $$ TS = \sum^N_{k=1}(SSV_k - BV_{nk}) * CCA_k + (SV_k - (SV_k - BV_{nk}))*CCA_k/2 $$ Where:

\(TS\) is the tax due on salvage
\(BV_{nk}\) is the book value of the \(k^{th}\) class in the \(n^{th}\) year that the class is closed
\(SV_k\) is the salvage value of the class of the \(k^{th}\) class Sample Calculation: Tax due on Salvage for Class 10 & 8 items $$ T_S = (62,500 - 6,000)*0.30 + (6,273 - 2,536)*0.20 = $17,561 $$


Net Salvage Value

Total money recovered after closing the book of k CCA classes less their respective taxes due $$ NSV = \sum^N_{k=1} [SV_k-TS] $$ Where \(NSV\) is the net salvage value Sample Calculation Net salvage value of class 10 & 8 $$ NSV = 62,500+6,273 - 17,561 = $50,939 $$


Evaluation

After Tax Cash Flow

The after tax cash flow is the sum of the before tax cash flow less the tax due $$ ATCF = BTCF - TI - TS $$ Where ATCF is the after tax cash flow Sample Calculation ATCF year 1 (no tax on salvage) $$ ATCF = 291,838 - 29,846 = $261,993 $$


Net Profit

The net profit is the taxable amount (including salvage) less the income tax $$ NP = TI - T - Ts $$ Where NP is the net profit Sample Calculation ATCF year 1 $$ NP = 248,716 - 29,846 = $21,8871 $$


Net Operating Funds

The net operating funds is the ATCF less the dividends or charities, working capital, and loan repayments $$ NOF = ATCF - WC - Di - A $$ Where

\(NOF\) is the net operating funds
\(WC\) is the working capital (taken as a negative value)
\(Di\) are dividends paid out or charitible funds donated to
note: We do not assume tax credits on charitable funds. Sample Calculation NOF year 1 $$ NOF = 26,1993 - 0 -15,2172 - 50,857 = $58,964 $$


Present Worth (at MARR)

The net present worth is the sum of present worth money over the sum of the lifespan of the project at the minimum acceptable interest rate (or rate of return) $$ PW = F(1+i)^{-n} $$ Where

\(PW\) is the present worth
\(i\) is the market interest rate
\(F\) is the future value
\(n\) is the number of years in the future
Sample Calculation PW year 1 at MARR 0.09 (where MARR is the minimum accetable interest rate) $$ PW = 58,964*(1.09)^{-1} = $54,095 $$


Net Present Worth

The present worth is the value of money today you would need to invest for \(n\) years into the future at interest rate \(i\) to obtain the future value $$ NPW = \sum^LS_{k=1} [PW_k] $$ Where

\(NPW\) is the net present worth
\(LS\) is the lifespan of the project (as an integer)
Sample Calculation NPW at MARR 0.09 (where MARR is the minimum acceptable interest rate) $$ NPW = -67,108-54,095+56,033+52,807... = $87,3476 $$
A positve NPW indicates that the project will return more money over its lifespan than a bond/stock at the same MARR


Rate of Return

The rate of return is interest rate at which the net present worth is equal to 0 $$ ROR = NPW(i) = 0 $$ Where

\(ROR\) is the rate of return
Sample Calculation: ROR The ROR is the NPW with the objective value set to 0 by varying the interest rate. using the the solver tool, the ROR for a sale price of $31 is 97.75%
