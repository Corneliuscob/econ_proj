Looking at the expected value, we get an IRR of 98.1%, well above our after-tax MARR. Calculating the risk or standard deviation of the expected value, we get a value of 14.47% which is much smaller than our expected value, indicating that this is a good venture.


As seen, in our most likely outlook looking at 3% of the total craft beer drinkers who purchase their craft beer privately and enjoy drinking at home at a price of $33 a month. Aside from our most pessimistic scenario, there is no issues with meeting our MARR. We will assume the most probable scenario will occur, 3% SAM at a monthly rate of $33 when performing calculations.


This model is sensitive as it relies heavily on the amount of subscriber base that is obtained. A shift in either more or less subscribers will require an economic reevaluation causing an increase or decrease of subscription price.


Our after-tax MARR is developed by looking at the TSX over the last ten years. Looking at the growth trend in the TSX, we found there was an increase in prices by 10%, giving us a market interest rate of 10%. We set this as our after-tax market interest rate. Since our cash flow is based on real numbers (inflation is assumed to increase at the same rate as costs and benefits), we deflate the market interest to obtain a real interest rate. Please refer to the appendix for this calculation. With an average inflation rate of 1.5% over the last 10 years (cite), we obtained an average real interest rate of 8.37% if one were to invest their money into the market instead of into our venture. Based on this real interest rate, we set our after-tax MARR at 9%, slightly above market rate to ensure we still turn a small profit even if our rate of return is equal to our MARR.
